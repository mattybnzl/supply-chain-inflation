# Why Your Grocery Bill Keeps Climbing

When fuel costs rise 50%, why does your grocery bill jump 35%? This interactive tool lets you see exactly how percentage-based margins at each stage of a supply chain compound upward — turning a single cost shock into a snowball that grows at every step between the farm and your fridge.

**[Open the live tool](https://mattybnzl.github.io/supply-chain-inflation/)** — no install, runs in your browser.

## What you can do

- **Drag the fuel slider** and watch costs cascade through 5–8 supply chain stages in real time
- **Switch between products** — local bread, imported butter, imported coffee — each with different chain lengths and real AU price anchors
- **Load crisis presets** based on real events: COVID freight surge, Ukraine grain disruption, Red Sea rerouting, Iran fuel crisis
- **Explore 6 visualisation modes**: side-by-side comparison, cost waterfall, snowball cascade, 18-month timeline, household monthly impact, and supplier cash runway stress
- **Toggle between % and $ profit models** to see how fixed-dollar margins would reduce the snowball effect
- **Adjust competition pressure** to model how market forces absorb or amplify shocks

## The core idea

Every business in a supply chain adds a percentage margin. When input costs rise, that percentage is applied to a larger base — so the dollar profit grows even though the margin percentage stays the same. The next business in the chain then marks up on *that* higher number. By the time it reaches the consumer, the compounding is significant. No one is gouging — the system structure does it automatically.
