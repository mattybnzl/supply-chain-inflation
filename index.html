The Journey of a Loaf of Bread

Every product you buy passes through multiple hands before it reaches you. At each stage, a business adds their costs and their profit. Traditionally, that profit is a percentage of total costs — so when costs rise, their dollar profit rises too, even if they didn't ask for more. Then the next business does the same, on top of the already-inflated price. This is the snowball effect.

It's not just consumers who feel the pressure. Every business in the chain must pay higher input costs upfront before they receive payment from the next buyer — often 30 to 90 days later. Smaller operators with thin cash reserves can be squeezed out entirely, breaking the chain for everyone.

What This Model Doesn't Capture

This is a simplified view of one mechanism. Real supply chains are messier — and that's OK. Understanding this one pattern helps explain a meaningful part of why prices rise faster than expected.

Competition

In competitive markets, businesses often absorb some cost increases to avoid losing customers. The competition slider above lets you explore this.

Wages & Other Costs

When fuel rises, so do wages, rent, and insurance. Businesses face real cost increases beyond fuel — the percentage model isn't pure greed.

Contracts & Buffers

Large buyers often have 3-6 month fixed-price contracts. Cost shocks don't hit consumers instantly — there's a lag this model doesn't show.

Currency Exchange

For imported goods, a weakening local currency amplifies costs independently of fuel — a second compounding layer not modelled here.

Government Intervention

Fuel excise cuts, agricultural subsidies, and strategic reserves all dampen cost pass-through in ways this model doesn't include.

Speculation

Commodity prices often spike on anticipated disruption, not actual shortages. The initial cost shock itself may be partly speculative.

How Does the Snowball Work?

  1. Each stage in the supply chain has operating costs and a fuel/energy sensitivity (shown as dots).
  2. When fuel costs rise, each stage's operating costs increase based on their fuel exposure.
  3. Under % profit, each stage applies their profit percentage to the new, higher total — so their dollar profit grows, unintentionally.
  4. Under $ profit, each stage adds the same dollar profit as before. Only actual cost increases pass through.
  5. The gap between the two is the snowball surcharge — price increase caused by the pricing method, not by actual costs.
Simple example: A baker buys flour for $1.00 and adds 50% profit = sells for $1.50 (profit: $0.50).
Flour rises to $1.40. With 50% profit: sells for $2.10 (profit: $0.70 — $0.20 extra they didn't ask for).
With fixed $ profit: sells for $1.90 (profit: $0.50 — same as before). You save $0.20.

Now imagine that happening 5 to 8 times, one business after another. Each one's increase feeds into the next one's base cost. That's the snowball.
About this model: This is a simplified educational tool showing one mechanism of price transmission through supply chains. Real pricing involves competition, regulation, contracts, wages, and many factors not shown here. Most businesses in the supply chain are small operators with tight margins — this model shows a systemic effect, not individual greed. The goal is to illustrate how percentage-based pricing can amplify cost increases, not to assign blame or prescribe policy. Prices shown are illustrative.
